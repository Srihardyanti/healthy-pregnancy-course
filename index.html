🧬 Chapter 1
Pregnancy as a Bio-Psycho-Social Process
Pregnancy can be understood using the bio-psycho-social model: Biological, Psychological, and Social.
These dimensions are interdependent — changes in one can influence the others.
🧬 Chapter 1
How These Dimensions Interact
🧬
Biological — physiological and hormonal changes: fatigue, nausea, sleep disruption, appetite changes driven by hormonal fluctuations and fetal development.
🧠
Psychological — thoughts, emotions, mental states: stress, anxiety, mood changes, coping ability. Pregnancy can act as a psychological stressor.
🤝
Social — relationships, support systems, family dynamics, cultural expectations, access to care. These shape emotional well-being and health behaviors.
🧬 Chapter 1
How They Are Interconnected
1
Psychological stress can activate biological responses (e.g., hormonal changes affecting sleep and appetite)
2
Social support can buffer emotional distress and improve coping capacity
3
Physical discomfort can influence mood and behavior
For example: stress (psychological) may disrupt sleep (biological), while limited support (social) can intensify emotional distress and reduce coping ability.

Understanding Pregnancy Challenges
Pregnancy challenges can be grouped into three interconnected domains: physical (body changes), emotional (mood and mental state), and relationship (roles and interactions).
These domains influence each other and shape the overall pregnancy experience.
🤰
Challenge 1: Physical
💪 Chapter 2
Physical Challenges
Pregnancy introduces physiological adaptations that support fetal development. These changes may result in symptoms such as nausea, fatigue, and discomfort.
💪 Chapter 2
Why Physical Symptoms Occur
Physical symptoms during pregnancy are driven by biological adaptations:
1
Progesterone (a hormone that maintains pregnancy) relaxes smooth muscles, including the digestive tract → slows gastric emptying (movement of food), causing nausea and bloating
2
Blood volume increases by ~30–24% to support oxygen and nutrient delivery → increases cardiovascular workload, contributing to fatigue and dizziness
3
Uterine growth (expansion of the womb) shifts internal organs and increases pressure → leading to discomfort and back pain
These are adaptive processes, but may still affect daily functioning.
Better Health Channel, n.d.; Sengupta et al., 2016

✅ Common

  • Mild nausea
  • Fatigue
  • Slight dizziness

⚠️ Warning Signs

  • Persistent vomiting (hyperemesis gravidarum — extreme nausea condition)
  • Severe headache (possible preeclampsia — high blood pressure disorder)
💪 Chapter 2
Normal vs Warning Signs
⚠️ Differences lie in severity, duration, and impact on daily life.
McCarthy et al., 2014; NHS, 2021

Challenge 2: Emotional
💪 Chapter 2
Emotional Challenges
Pregnancy involves emotional adjustments due to hormonal changes and life transitions. Anxiety, mood swings, and stress are common experiences during this period.
💪 Chapter 2
Why Emotional Changes Occur
Emotional changes during pregnancy are influenced by hormonal activity affecting brain function:
1
Estrogen (regulates reproductive processes) interacts with serotonin (a neurotransmitter related to mood) → contributing to mood swings
2
Progesterone (supports pregnancy) has a calming but sedative effect → may cause fatigue and emotional fluctuation
3
Cortisol (a hormone involved in stress response) naturally increases during pregnancy:
→ Function: supports fetal development, energy supply, and preparation for birth
→ Side effect: when levels are too high or prolonged, it may heighten anxiety and stress response
These hormonal changes interact with psychological and social factors, shaping emotional experiences.
Fan et al., 2009
💪 Chapter 2
Why Emotional Health Matters
Emotional distress during pregnancy has measurable health impacts:
• Around 10–20% of pregnant women experience anxiety or depression
• High stress is associated with preterm birth and low birth weight
Why this happens: When stress is prolonged, it activates the HPA axis (the body's stress-response system) in a sustained way:
→
This leads to continued cortisol release, rather than short-term regulation
→
Elevated cortisol can cross the placenta, influencing the fetal environment
→
Over time, this may affect fetal growth, stress regulation, and birth outcomes
At the same time, emotional distress also affects daily behaviors: poor sleep, reduced nutrition, lower engagement with healthcare.
These biological and behavioral pathways work together, increasing the overall risk to maternal and fetal health.
Howard et al., 2018; Jahan et al., 2021
⚡ QUIZ TIME
Understanding Emotional Patterns
A pregnant individual feels anxious and notices changes in sleep and energy. What does this situation show?
✅ Explanation
Correct: B
✅ Emotional stress affects both physical health and daily behavior.
This happens through two connected pathways:
1
Biological pathway (inside the body): Emotional stress activates the HPA axis (the body's stress-response system) → increases cortisol (stress hormone) → affects immune function, sleep regulation, and the uterine environment
2
Behavioral pathway (daily actions): Stress can change daily habits → such as poor sleep, reduced appetite, and avoiding healthcare
These biological and behavioral changes interact and may influence both maternal health and fetal development.
Howard et al., 2018; Jahan et al., 2021
💪 Chapter 2
Managing Emotional Well-being
1. Reducing the physiological stress response:
•
Physical activity and balanced nutrition help regulate neurotransmitters (e.g., serotonin) → improving mood stability
•
Breathing and relaxation techniques activate the parasympathetic nervous system → reducing stress hormone levels
2. Having a strong support system, associated with lower risk of prenatal depression and better well-being:
•
Sharing experiences reduces perceived stress and emotional burden
•
Support from partner/family improves coping ability
•
Professional support provides structured intervention

Challenge 3: Relational
💪 Chapter 2
Relationship Challenges
Pregnancy introduces real-life relational changes:
•
A pregnant individual may seek emotional reassurance, while their partner may focus on financial preparation
•
Differences in priorities can lead to misunderstanding and tension
•
External influences (family advice, cultural expectations) may add pressure
These dynamics are part of transitioning into parenthood, not just "conflict".
💪 Chapter 2
Why Relationship Changes Occur
Relationship strain during pregnancy is influenced by:
1
Role transition (partner → parent)
2
Changes in expectations and responsibilities
3
Communication gaps during stress adaptation
In pregnancy context, uncertainty and adjustment amplify these differences, increasing risk of stress and emotional strain.
Lawrence et al., 2007; Raisingchildren.net.au, n.d.

😞 No Support

  • Higher emotional distress
  • Poorer pregnancy experience
  • Reduced coping

💪 With Support

  • Reduced stress & anxiety
  • Better coping & resilience
  • Less physical burden
💪 Chapter 2
Why Support Matters
Support systems act as a protective factor; in contrast, lack of support is associated with higher emotional distress and poorer pregnancy experience:
•
Emotional support reduces stress perception and anxiety
•
Practical support (e.g., help with tasks) reduces physical and mental burden
•
Strong relationships improve coping and resilience
Battulga et al., 2021; Bedaso et al., 2021a

🚫 Myth: Exercise is Dangerous
💡 Chapter 3
Myth 2: Exercise is Dangerous
This myth stems from fear of miscarriage (loss of pregnancy before 44 weeks). Many people avoid exercise out of this unfounded fear.
💡 Chapter 3
Fact: Moderate Exercise is Safe & Beneficial
Regular physical activity helps reduce the risk of gestational diabetes (high blood sugar during pregnancy) and hypertension (high blood pressure) through several biological mechanisms:
✓
Improves insulin sensitivity (how the body responds to insulin, the hormone that regulates blood sugar) → helps use glucose more effectively and prevents blood sugar from rising too high
✓
Supports healthy weight gain → reduces excess fat accumulation, which is associated with higher risk of gestational diabetes
✓
Enhances cardiovascular function (heart and blood vessel performance) → improves blood circulation and helps regulate blood pressure
✓
Reduces inflammation and stress hormones (e.g., cortisol — a hormone released during stress) → helps stabilize both blood sugar and blood pressure levels
Regular moderate exercise contributes to better metabolic and cardiovascular health during pregnancy.
Barakat et al., 2019; Barakat et al., 2016; Haakstad et al., 2016; Mottola et al., 2018

💡 Chapter 3
Myth 3: Advanced Maternal Age
This myth assumes pregnancy above 35 is always unsafe — but health status and monitoring matter far more than age alone.
💡 Chapter 3
Fact: Risk Depends on Overall Health
Research shows that pregnancy after age 35 is associated with a higher likelihood of certain complications. However, age alone is not the only factor influencing these risks.
Many complications are linked to underlying conditions such as:
• Hypertension (high blood pressure)
• Diabetes (high blood sugar levels)
These conditions can also occur in younger pregnant women.
Important to maintain:
✓
Personalized health check-ups before and during pregnancy
✓
Maintaining a healthy lifestyle
When risk factors are properly managed and pregnancy is closely monitored, outcomes can still be physiologically healthy, without increased maternal or neonatal risk.
Bouzaglou et al., 2020; Lean et al., 2017
